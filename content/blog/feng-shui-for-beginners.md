## Start with Your Front Door

Your front door is where energy enters your home. In Feng Shui, it's called the "mouth of chi." If your door sticks, squeaks, or is blocked by clutter, you're literally blocking opportunity from walking in.

Here's the thing most people miss: the door itself matters less than what you see when you open it. If the first thing you see is a wall, a pile of shoes, or a dark hallway, that's the first impression your home's energy makes. Fix this before buying any crystals.

Quick checklist for your front door:
- Clean it. A dusty door with dead leaves around it = stagnant energy
- Make sure it opens fully. If something blocks it from swinging 90 degrees, move it
- The doormat matters. Replace a worn-out mat. Red is ideal — it's the color of welcoming energy
- If your door faces a bathroom, keep that bathroom door closed

One thing nobody tells you: a broken doorbell is worse than a missing crystal. It's the first thing visitors interact with. Fix it.

## Find Your Kua Number

Your Kua number is basically your personal Feng Shui fingerprint. It's calculated from your birth year and gender, and it tells you four lucky directions and four unlucky ones.

For most people, this takes 10 seconds with a calculator. The math is simple: add the last two digits of your birth year, keep adding until you get a single digit, then subtract from 10 if you're male or add 5 if you're female. But honestly, just use the tool — nobody does this manually.

What you actually do with the number: face your Sheng Chi direction (wealth) when you work. Face Tian Yi (health) when you're sick. Point your bed toward Yan Nian (relationships) if you're single. These are the only four directions that matter for your number, and they're different for everyone.

East Group people (Kua 1, 3, 4, 9) do best facing East, Southeast, South, or North. West Group (2, 6, 7, 8) do best facing West, Northwest, Southwest, or Northeast. If you've been sleeping facing the wrong direction for years, you'll notice a difference within a week of changing it.

## Clear the Clutter — But Be Smart About It

Clutter is the most boring Feng Shui advice and also the most important. Stagnant objects trap stagnant energy. If you haven't touched something in a year, your home's energy hasn't either.

But here's what the Instagram Feng Shui people won't tell you: you don't need to go full Marie Kondo. Focus on three areas:

1. Under your bed. This is the worst place for storage. Energy circulates around your body while you sleep, and boxes of old tax returns under the mattress block that flow. Keep it empty. Nothing. Not even shoes.

2. Entryway. The first 5 feet inside your front door. If you have to squeeze past coats and umbrellas to enter your home, that's the energy equivalent of showing up to a party and having to fight the bouncer.

3. Your desk. A cluttered desk = a cluttered mind. This one isn't mystical — it's neuroscience. Visual clutter forces your brain to process irrelevant information, draining focus.

The rule: if you use it weekly, it stays. If you use it monthly, it goes in a drawer. If you haven't used it in 6 months, donate it or toss it.

## Fix Your Bed Position

You spend a third of your life in bed. The position of your bed is arguably the single most impactful Feng Shui adjustment you can make.

The one rule that actually matters: command position. Your bed should be positioned so you can see the bedroom door without being directly in line with it. Not directly facing it. Not with your feet pointing at it (that's called the "coffin position" for a reason). Diagonally — so you can see who's coming in but you're not in the direct path.

If your bed is against a window: move it if you can. Windows drain energy. If you can't move it, use heavy curtains and keep them closed while you sleep.

Headboard against a solid wall: good. Headboard against a wall with plumbing pipes behind it: bad. The sound of water rushing through pipes while you're trying to sleep is genuinely disruptive — this one's just common sense.

What to put on your nightstand: one lamp, one book, one glass of water. That's it. Matching nightstands on both sides if you share the bed — asymmetry creates relationship imbalance (and yes, this one actually tracks).

## Add the 5 Elements — Without Buying Anything

Feng Shui uses five elements: Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water. A balanced home has all five present. The trick is you probably already own items that represent each one — you just need to place them correctly.

Wood: a living plant. Any plant. A $5 succulent from the grocery store counts. Place it in the East (family/health) or Southeast (wealth).

Fire: anything red. A red book cover facing out on your shelf. A red coffee mug. A candle, lit occasionally. Place in the South (fame/recognition).

Earth: anything ceramic, clay, or yellow. A terracotta pot. A brown throw pillow. Place in the center of your home or the Northeast/Southwest.

Metal: anything white, silver, or metallic. A metal picture frame. White walls count. A wind chime. Place in the West or Northwest.

Water: anything black, dark blue, or wavy. A black rug. A mirror (represents water). A fountain — but only if you're willing to maintain it. A dirty fountain is worse than no fountain.

You don't need to buy a $40 "Feng Shui crystal tree" to balance your elements. Walk around your house with this list and you'll find all five within 10 minutes.

## Check Your Wealth Corner

Every home has a wealth corner. It's always the far-left corner from the front door, facing in. This is the Xun position on the Bagua map, associated with abundance and prosperity.

What actually goes there: a healthy plant (preferably with round leaves — they represent coins), something purple or gold, and a moving element like a small fountain or even a screensaver with flowing water.

What definitely does NOT go there: a trash can. A pile of unopened mail. A dead plant. A bathroom (if your wealth corner is in the bathroom, keep the toilet lid closed and the door shut).

The most common mistake: people put a "wealth bowl" or "money frog" in this corner and call it done, while the corner is dusty and the plant is dying. The objects don't matter as much as the energy. A clean, intentional, living corner beats a dusty altar of expensive trinkets every time.

## What NOT to Do in Your First Week

The internet is full of Feng Shui advice that ranges from unhelpful to actively counterproductive. Here's what to skip:

Don't buy a Bagua mirror unless you know what you're doing. A convex Bagua mirror placed incorrectly can reflect good energy away from your home. These are defensive tools, not decorations.

Don't hang wind chimes randomly. Different materials suppress different energies. A metal wind chime in the wrong sector can suppress beneficial stars. If you don't know your Flying Star chart, skip the wind chimes.

Don't put a fountain in your bedroom. Water in the bedroom creates excessive yang energy and can lead to restlessness and financial loss. The bedroom should be the most yin (calm) room in the house.

Don't mix too many "cures" at once. Pick 1-2 things from this guide and stick with them for a month. Feng Shui is about observing how changes affect your life. If you change 10 things at once, you'll never know what worked.

And please: don't spend $200 on crystals your first week. The free adjustments on this page will do more for your home's energy than any amount of amethyst.
